## Storylines to Watch

### 1. The LSU Seed Anomaly: The Best Team in the Field Is the Fourth Seed

This is the defining storyline of the 2026 SEC Tournament. By every efficiency metric that matters — Net Efficiency (+45.8, best in field), ORtg (121.8, best in field), and SRS (46.93, second in field) — LSU is the best or second-best team in Greenville. They are seeded fourth because their conference record (11-4) trails the 12-3 marks of both Texas and Vanderbilt.

The catch: LSU's SOS of 11.11 is the second-lowest in the field, which means those dominant numbers were constructed against softer competition. Two agents in this pipeline flag the discrepancy and reach conflicting conclusions. Sub-Agent 3 (Matchup Analyst) leans toward South Carolina's schedule-adjusted superiority in the semifinal. Sub-Agent 4 (Upset Detector) calls the LSU-vs.-field matchup "essentially a coin flip" and explicitly flags the SOS caveat as unresolved. This disagreement is not reconciled here — it is the right question, and it will be answered on the court Saturday afternoon.

What is not in dispute: LSU's path — a quarterfinal against an Oklahoma/Mississippi State winner, then a likely semifinal against Texas — is manageable enough that they will probably get to test their ceiling against a top seed. If their ORB% (48.8%, nearly half of all missed shots becoming second chances) holds up against elite competition, the bracket is blown open.

### 2. Tennessee's Ticking Clock: The Most Overseeded Team in the Field

Tennessee is a 7-seed with the statistical profile of an 11. Their Net Efficiency (+11.7) is the lowest of any team seeded 1 through 8, by a substantial margin. Their ORtg (101.5) and TS% (.507) are both below the field median. Their DRtg (89.8) is the worst among top-eight seeds.

The defense: Tennessee played the hardest schedule in the entire tournament field (SOS 19.77 — nearly two full points above the second-hardest). Every raw number they post was earned against elite competition. Their 16-11 record, though unimpressive on its face, tells a different story when you account for who they played.

But here is the problem with that defense in single-elimination basketball: schedule credit evaporates at tipoff. What remains is shooting (.507 TS%), defensive efficiency (89.8 DRtg), and a three-point dependency (3PAr .457 — highest in the field) that makes them one cold-shooting game away from elimination. Georgia in the Second Round will test them immediately.

### 3. Georgia's Quiet Case for a Semifinal Run

Georgia is a 10-seed that no one is talking about. They should be. Their Net Efficiency (+18.0) ranks eighth in the field — ahead of Tennessee (11th), Alabama (9th), Mississippi State (10th), and every other team seeded below them. Their DRtg (84.5) is the best of any team outside the top six seeds. They will beat Auburn in the First Round without much drama, then face Tennessee in the Second Round — a game where every efficiency metric favors Georgia despite the seed disadvantage.

The caveat, which this report will not bury: Georgia's SOS (10.89) is the second-lowest in the field. Their defensive numbers may not hold up against competition above the level they faced in the regular season. Sub-Agent 2 flags this explicitly, Sub-Agent 4 rates the Georgia-over-Tennessee upset at HIGH probability despite acknowledging the schedule concern. The correct answer is that both things are true: Georgia is statistically better than their seed, and their metrics were built on a soft schedule. They will be a live underdog against Texas in the quarterfinals regardless of which version of Georgia shows up.

### 4. South Carolina vs. Texas: The Championship Game No One Should Argue With

The two teams that make the most statistical sense for the championship are the top two seeds, and the most likely outcome is a championship game that looks exactly like it should. South Carolina (ORtg 118.6, DRtg 76.1, SOS 16.66) and Texas (ORtg 116.3, DRtg 75.7, SOS 16.39) are statistically indistinguishable. The Net Efficiency gap is 1.9 points. The schedule difference is 0.27 SOS points. Both teams lead the field in defensive efficiency and play interior-dominant basketball at identical paces (73.9 vs. 73.7 possessions per 40 minutes).

The Matchup Analyst rates this the "most statistically balanced final possible" and gives South Carolina the edge solely on shooting efficiency (.587 vs. .559 TS%). Sub-Agent 2 calls it "a coin flip." This report agrees with both assessments. South Carolina wins on a tiebreaker that could reasonably go the other way.

### 5. The Bottom-Bracket Battle: Who Escapes?

Seeds 9 through 13 represent the tournament's most interesting chaos zone. Ole Miss (8) is a genuine defensive team with elite offensive rebounding (40.4% ORB%). Alabama (9) shoots the ball better than their opponent (.542 vs. .516 TS%) and comes in with a chip on their shoulder after a losing conference record. Georgia (10) is statistically better than their seed. Kentucky (6) is a three-point-shooting team that can go cold without warning. Tennessee (7) is a three-point-shooting team that is already going cold on an efficiency basis.

In a bracket where the top four seeds feel inevitable, the most competitive basketball over the first two rounds may come from the matchups nobody is circling on their brackets.

South Carolina wins 62-58. It could just as easily be 62-58 the other way.

## Data Note

All statistics used in this report derive from the local fallback dataset at `/data/sec_teams.csv`, which was generated by `scripts/build_data.py` by scraping Sports Reference's 2025-26 Women's Advanced School Stats and Advanced Opponent Stats pages. The bracket structure — dates, times, networks, and matchup pairings — is fixed in the bracket.json file; only the seed-to-team mapping is algorithmically derived. Sub-Agent 1's data audit confirmed no imputation was required and flagged Texas A&M's 24-game sample (vs. 27-30 for all other teams) as a minor uncertainty flag carried through all downstream analysis.
